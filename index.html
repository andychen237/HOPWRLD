PROBLEM SPACE AND DESIGN FOCUS

The pandemic has effectively changed the landscape of college education and campus life as we know it. With the move to remote education, almost all of our academic and social life is based on some virtual meeting platform, and if you're the average college student, you most likely live and breathe Zoom in your daily life. Since these platforms were not specifically designed for supporting our daily quarantine life, with this "new normal" that was thrown upon us, there still remains a significant gap between studying and socializing virtually and physically. Whether it's Zoom fatigue, cabin fever, or the unbearable pain of not being able to see your favorite CharMar lady, being a student during these trying times is certainly tough.

Therefore, we introduce a brand-new VR platform, HOPWRLD, that will expand beyond the walls of a classroom to provide an immersive, interactive environment to better engage college students academically and socially. We believe that with the VR platform, all of us can not only learn better, but also live a better life during this unprecedented time (all without having to hear "Can you all see my screen?" from a professor ever again).

DESIGN PROCESS

User Research Process

For a user research study that comprehensively covers the student experience, we decided to divide the college setting into three key focus areas: the classroom lecture experience, the lab experience, and the social experience (e.g. meetings, extracurriculars, etc.). To gain a better understanding of how things operate virtually under these conditions, we decided to do a contextual inquiry of three different users with a different college setting for each respective user and also sent out questionnaires that covered these key aspects of the college experience.

Contextual Inquiry

For the contextual inquiry, we decided to take a mostly hands-off approach and simply watch each student in their "natural habitat" uninterrupted because we thought that would produce an authentic record of how students normally interact with these virtual operations in their day-to-day. Thus, it was more of an open-exploration study as opposed to a task-oriented one, and we simply just recorded observations and asked questions as see fit.

Case study: Lecture meeting
This was quite a short-lived contextual inquiry because the user gave up on the task within ten minutes! The user was attending an asynchronous lecture of one of her upper-level classes for major. She had actually attended the live lecture earlier, but she could not stay focused and got lost during the lecture. Therefore, she was actually revisiting this lecture to both consolidate what she had learned and catch up on what she got lost on.

User Questionnaire

We also created a questionnaire that 8 users filled out to achieve a better idea of how users feel about current virtual education operations. This supplemented our contextual inquiry, which mostly consisted of observations, and gave us explicit feedback and ideas that we might not have garnered from solely observations. Analysis & Ideation

From the contextual inquiry observations and the user questionnaire, we saw many running themes.

- Users value interactions with other users the most, otherwise they tend to feel too distracted during the Zoom meeting because of the difference of not being in a traditional classroom setting. They suggest more interactions with the professors and amongst students themselves to mitigate this lack of engagement.



- Most users (62.5%) have not been able to make any new friends since remote classes and of those who answered the questions about extracurriculars, none participate in extracurriculars, which shows a severely lacking social experience during this time.

- The lab experience is lacking in hands-on experience and group work, which is hindering the learning process and level of engagement during these classes. During these limiting times, unfortunately, laboratory work is limited to purely video demos and this is a huge pitfall for those looking to gain from the laboratory experience.

A running theme within our research is the pure fact that none of the students were focused or engaged.

After analyzing the results of our user research, we did a group brainstorm together using a Miro board to come up with solutions to the running problems we saw.
